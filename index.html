About Us

Hang Yu, Assistant Professor

Department of Materials Science and Engineering

Virginia Tech

Blacksburg, VA 24061

Professor Hang Yu earned his B.S. in Physics from Peking University in 2007, and his Ph.D. in Materials Science and Engineering from Massachusetts Institute of Technology in 2013. Professor Yu is expert in the areas of advanced manufacturing and mechanical behavior of materials. He will start his research group in the Department of Materials Science and Engineering at Virginia Tech in January 2016.

Yu Research Group develops Emerging Structural and Smart Materials with unprecedented properties, such as ultra high actuation energy density, damping capacity, impact resistance, and ultra low weight. We focus on materials design at the meso-scale and micro-scale by exploring opportunities arising at the triple point between additive manufacturing, materials science, and mechanics modeling. As materials scientists, we are especially interested in using experiments and simulations to understand the mechanisms underlying the remarkable materials behavior.

Mesoscale materials design in additive manufacturing (3D printing)

Additive manufacturing (AM) offers unique capabilities that are unobtainable by traditional machining-based manufacturing methods. The most exploited capability of AM so far is the control of complex shapes in 3D. From a material perspective, it is important to note that multi-material AM also allows for the control of material meso-structure, i.e. meso-scale distribution of composition, microstructure and properties. Meso-structure optimization will lead to dramatic efficiencies in materials usage, damage tolerance, weight savings and other figures of merit. While topology optimization (macro-scale) and microstructure optimization (micro-scale) have been extensively explored, meso-structure optimization (meso-scale) has not been approached previously, as conventional manufacturing technologies produce monolithic materials. The advent of multi-material AM will enable the use of this new opportunity...

Shape memory ceramics and relevant composites

Shape memory and superelasticity are two unique properties of shape memory alloys (SMAs) that originate from reversible martensitic transformations between two different crystallographic phases. While single crystal SMAs have extraordinary properties for energy damping and actuation, they are expensive and difficult to process; polycrystalline SMAs, on the other hand, often fail at a low strain level caused by the mismatch stresses during martensitic transformation. The stress accommodation problem is especially profound in materials for high temperature applications, such as shape memory ceramics (SMC) due to their brittle nature. While the mismatch stresses may be reduced in small size samples, large-scale utilization of SMC-based applications remains elusive, as it is hard to do so in macroscopic manufacturing. One solution is to develop polymer and metal matrix composites using single crystal shape memory ceramic powders or 3D skeleton structures as the fillers...
